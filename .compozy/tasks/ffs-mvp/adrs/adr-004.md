# ADR-004: Three motivating scenarios all in MVP

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-05-05

## Context

Three motivating scenarios drive FFS's design:

- **Contact-graph sovereignty.** Salespeople, journalists, founders, and community organizers owning their relationship networks independently of platforms that can revoke access. The Folodex use case.
- **Home-claw absorption.** Power users with many AI agents writing markdown to disk; the substrate absorbs sloppy multi-writer ingestion and produces structured claims.
- **Day-one-clone-and-collaborate.** A new colleague clones the company's substrate from git, becomes productive immediately, contributes back without merge conflicts, sees only what they're entitled to via capability filtering.

A YAGNI consideration is whether all three need to ship in MVP, or whether MVP can focus on one (most likely contact-graph sovereignty, given the success criterion) and defer the others.

## Decision

All three motivating scenarios ship in MVP. The substrate's primitives — atoms with signing, classification, capability evaluation, federation, projection rendering — are needed for every one of them. Cutting a scenario doesn't reduce implementation work; it just removes a use case the substrate already supports.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Contact-graph sovereignty only

- **Description:** Focus MVP on the Folodex use case. Defer home-claw absorption and clone-and-collaborate to Phase 2.
- **Pros:** Sharpest match to success criterion. Less surface to validate. Easier marketing story.
- **Cons:** The substrate primitives must exist anyway; the scenarios are different applications of the same primitives. "Cutting" a scenario in this case means not validating that the substrate works for that use case, not actually saving implementation work.
- **Why rejected:** The architectural primitives required for all three scenarios are the same. The difference is which surfaces and validation flows are exercised. Validating breadth in MVP is more credible than validating one slice.

### Alternative 2: Substrate primitives only with one minimal app surface

- **Description:** Ship the substrate with a minimal surface (CLI only, no Obsidian plugin). All three scenarios become Phase 2 when surfaces are built out.
- **Pros:** Smallest possible MVP. Sharpest separation of substrate from applications.
- **Cons:** Both audiences first-class (ADR-002) requires the Obsidian plugin in MVP. Cutting it is incompatible with the audience commitment. Without the plugin, the end-user surface doesn't exist and the small-community success criterion can't be tested.
- **Why rejected:** Conflicts with ADR-002. The substrate without surfaces can't be used; surfaces are part of MVP.

## Consequences

### Positive

- MVP validates breadth of architectural claims. The substrate is shown to handle contacts, agent absorption, and team collaboration with the same primitives — which is the actual claim being made.
- Users with different primary use cases can find their reason to adopt. The salesperson cares about contacts; the power user cares about agent absorption; the team member cares about clone-and-collaborate. MVP serves all three.
- Phase 2 priorities emerge from real use across all three scenarios, not from one slice.

### Negative

- More validation flows to test. More surface to support during onboarding. More documentation to write.
- Each scenario gets less depth than it would under a single-scenario focus.

### Risks

- Spreading attention across three scenarios may mean none is fully realized. **Mitigation:** Substrate-First MVP (ADR-003) keeps all three at functional-but-not-polished; depth is Phase 2 work.

## Implementation Notes

Validation flows for MVP must include scenarios from all three. The starter path library (contacts, people, notes — see ADR-011) supports all three with the same paths used differently: contacts/people for relationship-graph use cases, notes for general capture and agent absorption.
